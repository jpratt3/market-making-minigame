What is this game?
You are a market maker on an options desk. Your job is to quote prices to clients who want to buy or sell options — and to make money from the spread between your bid and ask price.

Every few seconds a client arrives wanting to buy or sell an option on a stock. You see the contract details, assess whether it's priced fairly, quote your bid and ask, and decide whether to fill the order. After filling, you manage your Greeks exposure — especially delta — to avoid taking too much directional risk.
Reading an Incoming Order
Each order shows you the following information:
Client wants to BUY / SELL
Whether the client is buying or selling the option. This determines which side of your quote they hit — buys hit your ask, sells hit your bid.
Strike & Expiry
The strike price ($175–$195) and days to expiry (7, 14, 30, or 60 days). These determine the option's value and Greeks.
Suggested Price
The price the client is showing. This may be above or below fair value — your job is to spot the difference and price accordingly.
Fair Value & Cheap/Rich
Black-Scholes fair value and whether the suggested price is cheap or rich vs fair. Rich + client buying = good sell. Cheap + client selling = good buy.
Greeks Preview
Shows how filling this order would affect your portfolio delta, and what theta and vega exposure you'd be taking on.
Competing Bot Quotes
Four competing bots also quote the market. To win the fill, your ask must be lower than theirs (client buy) or your bid higher (client sell).
Quoting Bid & Ask
You quote two prices simultaneously — a bid (price you'll buy at) and an ask (price you'll sell at). The spread between them is your edge per trade.

When a client wants to buy, they hit your ask. When they want to sell, they hit your bid. You only need to enter the relevant side — the game highlights which button to press.
Example: Fair value = $5.00. You quote $4.95 bid / $5.08 ask. Client wants to buy → hits your ask at $5.08 → you sold $0.08 above fair → +$8 edge per contract (100 shares × $0.08).
Managing Delta Exposure
Every option you fill adds delta to your portfolio — directional exposure to the stock price. If you don't hedge, a stock move can wipe out all your spread income.

The Stock Hedge panel shows your net delta in share equivalents. Use Flatten Δ to automatically trade the shares needed to neutralise it, or Buy/Sell 50 shares manually.
Example: You sold 3 calls with delta 0.5 each = −150 delta (short). Stock rises $1 → you lose $150. Buy 150 shares to flatten → now neutral. Stock can move freely without hurting you.
Scoring & P&L
Your total P&L has two components:
Spread Income
Edge captured from quoting — the difference between your fill price and fair value. This should always be positive if you're quoting correctly.
Greeks P&L
P&L from your options positions moving with the market — delta, gamma, theta, vega. Keep this small through disciplined hedging.
A good market maker has large positive spread income and Greeks P&L near zero. If your Greeks P&L is large and negative, you're taking too much directional risk and not hedging enough.
Market Events
On Medium difficulty and above, random events fire during the session — IV spikes, flash crashes, earnings surprises, vol crushes, and sector shocks. These simulate real market shocks you'd face on a trading desk.

On Easy and Medium, a banner appears with advice on how to respond. On Hard and Very Hard, the banner shows what happened but you must decide how to react yourself.
Live Payoff Diagram
The chart below the order card shows your portfolio's P&L at option expiry across a range of stock prices. Use the toggles to show strike markers, break-even prices, today's BS-priced value vs expiry intrinsic value, and individual position lines.

This tells you instantly: if the stock ends up at $X, how much do I make or lose?

Greek Delta Δ
▼
Delta measures how much an option's price moves when the stock moves $1. A call with delta 0.5 gains $0.50 when the stock rises $1. A put with delta −0.4 gains $0.40 when the stock falls $1.

As a market maker, your net portfolio delta tells you your total directional exposure. Long delta = you profit when the stock rises. Short delta = you profit when it falls. The goal is to stay near zero through hedging.
At Optiver: If you're asked "what's your delta?" they mean: how many shares equivalent of directional risk are you carrying right now?
Greek Gamma Γ
▼
Gamma measures how fast your delta changes when the stock moves. High gamma means a $1 move in the stock significantly changes your delta — your hedge goes stale quickly and needs frequent adjustment.

Short gamma (selling options) means large moves in either direction hurt you — your delta moves away from neutral faster than you can hedge. Long gamma (buying options) means large moves help you.
In practice: A short gamma MM who doesn't hedge after a flash crash can see losses compound rapidly as delta drifts further from neutral with each stock move.
Greek Theta Θ
▼
Theta measures how much an option loses in value each day from time passing. Options have a time premium that erodes daily — by expiry, they're worth only their intrinsic value.

Selling options earns positive theta — you collect this daily decay. Buying options pays negative theta — time works against you. Most MMs are net short options and earn theta as a steady income stream.
The tradeoff: Positive theta from selling options comes with short vega exposure. If IV spikes, your short options become more expensive to buy back — the theta you earned can be wiped out quickly.
Greek Vega ν
▼
Vega measures how much an option's value changes per 1% move in implied volatility. If your vega is −$15 and IV rises 1%, you lose $15.

Selling options makes you short vega. If the market becomes fearful and IV rises — like during an IV Spike event — short vega positions lose money even if the stock doesn't move.
Key insight: Theta and vega are linked. Selling options gives you positive theta but negative vega. You're paid daily decay in exchange for taking on the risk of IV rising against you.
Volatility Implied Volatility IV
▼
Implied volatility is the market's expectation of how much the stock will move, expressed as an annualised percentage. It's called "implied" because it's derived from current option prices — you work backwards from the price to find what volatility the market is pricing in.

Higher IV = more expensive options. IV spikes during uncertainty (before earnings, during crises) and crushes after events resolve. The fair IV in this game is 25% — meaning the market expects the stock to move roughly 25% per year.
In the game: When an IV Spike event fires, IV jumps from 25% to 40–60%. All options become more expensive. If you're short options, your positions are now worth more to buy back — a loss.
Volatility IV Surface / Vol Smile
▼
In reality, different options on the same stock trade at different IVs depending on their strike and expiry. This creates a "surface" of IVs across strikes and expiries.

The smile/skew: OTM puts trade at higher IV than ATM options because funds pay a premium for crash protection. OTM calls also trade at slightly elevated IV. The ATM strike has the lowest IV — this is the "volatility smile".

Term structure: Short-dated options often carry higher IV than long-dated in stressed markets. On Hard/VH, you use the surface to price orders without being told the fair value directly.
Reading the surface: If the $175 put (OTM) shows 28% IV and the $185 ATM shows 24% IV, the put has a 4% vol premium. An order showing 30% IV for the $175 put is rich — you should sell it.
Market Making Bid/Ask Spread & Edge
▼
The bid is the price you'll buy at. The ask is the price you'll sell at. The spread between them is your profit margin per round-trip trade.

Edge is the difference between your fill price and the option's fair value. Buy below fair value or sell above fair value — that's positive edge. Do the opposite — that's negative edge, and you're giving money away.

The tighter you quote vs bots, the more fills you win — but with less edge per fill. Too wide, and bots undercut you and take every order.
Example: Fair value $5.00. You quote $4.94 / $5.06. Client buys at your ask $5.06 → edge = $5.06 − $5.00 = $0.06 × 100 shares = $6 per contract.
Market Making Market Making
▼
A market maker continuously quotes prices to buy and sell — providing liquidity to the market. Unlike a directional trader who bets on where the market goes, a MM profits from the spread between bid and ask regardless of direction.

The risk: you don't choose your customers. Sometimes you're trading against someone who knows something you don't — an informed trader. Your edge comes from quoting many orders and law of large numbers, not from any single trade.
At Optiver/IMC: Traders quote thousands of options simultaneously, managing Greeks across their entire book in real time. The game simulates this one order at a time — in reality it's all at once.
Market Making Delta Hedging
▼
Delta hedging means trading the underlying stock to neutralise your options' directional exposure. If your options give you +80 delta (long exposure), you sell 80 shares to get back to neutral.

After hedging, small stock moves don't hurt you — your options gain/lose roughly what your stock position loses/gains. You then earn from gamma (if long) or theta (if short) rather than from the stock's direction.
Why it matters: Without hedging, you're just a directional trader with options. With hedging, you isolate the spread income and vol exposure — the actual MM edge — from random stock movements.
Risk Toxic Flow & Informed Trading
▼
Toxic flow is order flow from informed traders — people who know something about the stock's future direction that you don't. They consistently buy calls before rallies and puts before crashes, picking you off every time you fill their orders.

The tell is a pattern: if you see 3+ consecutive call buys or put buys, someone may be informed. Real MMs widen their spreads or stop quoting when they detect toxic flow to avoid being systematically picked off.
In the game: On Medium difficulty, the game warns you when it detects suspicious directional flow. On Very Hard, you must detect it yourself and decide whether to keep quoting or pass.

How to Play
Reading an Order
Each order shows you: the contract (call or put, strike, expiry), whether the client wants to buy or sell, the suggested price, and the Black-Scholes fair value. The CHEAP/RICH label tells you whether the suggested price is above or below fair value — and combined with the client's direction, tells you whether filling is good or bad for you.
Quoting Bid & Ask
You quote two prices simultaneously — a bid (price you'll buy at) and an ask (price you'll sell at). A client buying hits your ask. A client selling hits your bid. To beat the bots: quote your ask lower than theirs on client buys, your bid higher on client sells. The spread between your bid and ask is your edge per trade.
Example: Fair value $5.00. You quote $4.94 / $5.06. Client buys at your ask $5.06 → edge = $0.06 × 100 shares = +$6 per contract.
Delta Hedging
Every fill adds delta — directional exposure to the stock. If you don't hedge, stock moves hurt you. Use Flatten Δ to automatically trade the shares needed to neutralise your delta, or use the +50 / −50 buttons for manual hedges. The goal: earn from spread income, not from lucky stock moves.
Scoring
Total P&L = Spread Income + Greeks P&L. Spread income is edge captured from good fills. Greeks P&L is from your positions moving with the market. A good market maker has large positive spread income and Greeks P&L near zero from disciplined hedging.
